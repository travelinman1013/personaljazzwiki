## Biography
Wanda Jackson, born October 20, 1937, in Maud, Oklahoma, is a renowned American singer-songwriter known for her contributions to rockabilly, country, and gospel music. She began her music career at a young age, performing on a local radio show in Oklahoma City. Her talent caught the attention of Hank Thompson, who helped her secure a recording contract with Decca Records in 1954. Her early hits included 'You Can't Have My Love,' which became a national Top 10 country hit. In 1956, she signed with Capitol Records, where she explored both country and rockabilly styles, earning her the nickname 'The Queen of Rockabilly.' Her rockabilly singles, such as 'Fujiyama Mama' and 'Let's Have a Party,' gained significant popularity. In the 1960s, Jackson shifted her focus back to country music, achieving several charting singles. She later transitioned to gospel music in the 1970s. Jackson's legacy includes being inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame in 2009. She retired from performing in 2019 at the age of 81. Throughout her career, Jackson has been celebrated for her pioneering role in rock and roll and country music as a female artist. Her influence extends across genres, inspiring generations of musicians with her unique style and perseverance in a male-dominated industry. Her contributions to music have been recognized with numerous awards and inductions, solidifying her place as a music icon.
